About Josh

I am a graduate student working with Roman Grigoriev in the Center for Non-Linear Science at the Georgia Institute of Technology. My research interests include non-linear dynamics, pattern formation, as well as coherent structures and extreme behavior in turbulence. So many other fields of science have robust conceptual frameworks to pull from. Non-linear field theories, in general, lack a well-understood “toolbox” and for that reason, their dynamics is an incredibly exciting area of study. It’s just waiting for us to make the right change in perspective and understand it. To date, reduced-order models are our best bet, which is why I study pattern formation and exact coherent structure theory. Using these tools, I try to make sense of how the world around me works.

While my work now is entirely theoretical and computational, it did not start that way. My science career began as an experimental physicist at Princeton University, working with Frank Calaprice and the SABRE collaboration. During my time there, I learned how to design and build scientific systems (to be radioactively pure, chemically resistant, airtight…the list goes on and on). I got to probe the cosmos for dark matter through building a crystal-scintillating detector that was designed to emit light every time a dark matter particle collided with it. I even got to travel Italy with the collaboration, and work on the Darkside insertion system, nestled deep underneath the Gran Sasso mountain just outside of L’Aquila. My time at Princeton University was the first time I ever got to explore the world around me and build contraptions that could ask it how it works. It made me want to be a scientist.

While my work as an experimentalist was incredibly fulfilling, I was frustrated that company lead-times and technical spreadsheets were getting in the way of my science so often. I knew that I wanted to work in theory, where it would be just me grappling with the physics itself. That’s why, in my junior year of undergrad, I walked right into Stefan Boettcher’s office and asked to work with him on his research. With Stefan, I studied the asymptotic (in time) dynamics of Quantum Walks and got my first taste of non-linear dynamics. After a few long nights hunched over my laptop and scribbled pieces of paper, I had my first publication.

As I entered into grad school, I transitioned from dynamics on finite-dimensional systems to dynamics on infinite-dimensional systems (as if the former wasn’t hard enough). I now spend my time coding and scribbling tiny proofs on the scrap pieces of paper that line my desk, trying to understand the world around me better. I absolutely love the work I do.

My pronouns are He/Him/His, and while I’ve never been misgendered, or discriminated against, many incredible people in this community have been. Social justice is very close to my heart, and if there’s any way I can help make science more inclusive, please reach out.
